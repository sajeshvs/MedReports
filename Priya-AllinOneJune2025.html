What is Transferrin Saturation?

Shows how much iron is actually being transported to tissues. It's the percentage of transferrin (iron transport protein) that carries iron.

Your Level: Bone marrow is starved of iron.

Critical Interpretation

Saturation < 15% indicates functional iron deficiency. At 9.1%, your bone marrow cannot get enough iron to produce healthy red blood cells, even if some iron is circulating.

Related Values

  • TIBC: 378 µg/dL (High)
  • Calculation: 34.4/378 × 100 = 9.1%

What This Means

  • Low iron in blood
  • High binding capacity (body trying to catch more iron)
  • Very low saturation = iron starvation

Why So Low?

  • Depleted iron stores (ferritin 6.7)
  • Inadequate iron absorption
  • Ongoing iron loss
  • Poor dietary iron sources
  • Iron absorption inhibitors (tea, coffee)

  • Decreased oxygen transport
  • Impaired cellular energy production

Hemoglobin Drop Alert Protocol

×

Critical Red Flag

Definition: Hemoglobin dropping >0.5 g/dL after 4 weeks of iron therapy indicates treatment failure or ongoing blood loss. This requires immediate investigation for occult bleeding sources.

Expected vs. Concerning Response

Expected: Hb ↑ 1-2 g/dL
Red Flag: Hb ↓ >0.5 g/dL

Timeline: After 4 weeks of adequate iron therapy

Significance: Suggests ongoing blood loss exceeding production

Immediate Actions Required

  • Stop waiting: Don't continue iron alone
  • Urgent labs: CBC, reticulocyte count, LDH
  • Stool testing: 3 consecutive FIT/FOBT tests
  • GI consultation: Within 1-2 weeks

Comprehensive Bleeding Work-up

  • Upper GI: EGD (endoscopy) - check for ulcers, gastritis, varices
  • Lower GI: Colonoscopy - screen for polyps, IBD, malignancy
  • Small bowel: Capsule endoscopy if upper/lower normal
  • Gynecologic: Detailed menstrual history, pelvic exam
  • Urologic: Cystoscopy if persistent hematuria

Common Hidden Bleeding Sources

  • Peptic ulcers: H. pylori, NSAIDs, stress
  • Colorectal: Polyps, early malignancy, IBD
  • Small intestine: Crohn's, angiodysplasia
  • Gynecologic: Heavy menses, fibroids, polyps
  • Medications: Anticoagulants, aspirin
  • Systemic: Coagulopathy, platelet dysfunction

Investigation Timeline

  • Day 1-3: Stool occult blood x3, CBC, coags
  • Week 1: GI consultation, EGD scheduled
  • Week 2: Upper endoscopy performed
  • Week 3: Colonoscopy if upper normal
  • Week 4: Capsule endoscopy if needed

Additional Laboratory Assessment

  • Hemolysis screen: LDH, haptoglobin, indirect bilirubin
  • Coagulation studies: PT/INR, aPTT, platelet function
  • H. pylori testing: Stool antigen or breath test
  • Celiac screening: tTG-IgA (malabsorption cause)
  • B12/folate levels: Rule out combined deficiencies

Specialist Referral Criteria

  • Gastroenterology: Any GI bleeding suspicion (urgent)
  • Gynecology: Heavy menstrual bleeding patterns
  • Hematology: If hemolysis or bleeding disorder suspected
  • Surgery: If structural lesions requiring intervention

Emergency Criteria

Seek immediate medical attention if: Hb drops >2 g/dL, symptomatic anemia (chest pain, severe SOB), active bleeding signs (melena, hematochezia), or hemodynamic instability. Don't wait for scheduled follow-up.

Severe Hypocalcemia Emergency Protocol

×

Life-Threatening Emergency

Critical Threshold: Serum calcium <8.0 mg/dL (current: 8.48 mg/dL) can cause severe neuromuscular instability, tetany, seizures, and cardiac arrhythmias requiring immediate IV treatment.

Emergency Symptoms

  • Neuromuscular: Tetany, muscle spasms
  • Neurologic: Seizures, altered mental status
  • Cardiovascular: QT prolongation, arrhythmias
  • Respiratory: Laryngospasm, stridor

Physical Signs

  • Chvostek's sign: Facial twitching when tapped
  • Trousseau's sign: Hand spasm with BP cuff
  • Paresthesias: Perioral, fingertip tingling
  • Carpopedal spasm: Hand/foot cramping

Emergency Room Protocol

  • Immediate: IV access, cardiac monitoring, O2 saturation
  • Labs: Ionized calcium, magnesium, phosphorus, albumin, PTH
  • ECG: Check for QT prolongation, arrhythmias
  • IV calcium: 1-2 ampules calcium gluconate in 50-100ml NS over 10-20 min
  • Repeat labs: Calcium level after initial treatment

IV Calcium Protocol

  • First line: Calcium gluconate 1-2g (10-20ml of 10%)
  • Dilution: In 50-100ml normal saline
  • Rate: Over 10-20 minutes (not IV push)
  • Monitoring: Continuous cardiac monitoring
  • Repeat: Every 6 hours as needed

Adjunctive Treatments

  • Magnesium: 1-2g IV if Mg <1.5 mg/dL
  • Vitamin D: Calcitriol 0.25-0.5 mcg BID
  • Oral calcium: 1-2g elemental calcium daily
  • Seizure control: Benzodiazepines if needed

Precipitating Factors

  • Vitamin D deficiency: Most common cause
  • Hypoparathyroidism: Post-surgical, autoimmune
  • Magnesium deficiency: Impairs PTH function
  • Medications: Loop diuretics, bisphosphonates
  • Malabsorption: Celiac, Crohn's disease
  • Rapid correction: Phosphate administration

Cardiac Complications

  • QT prolongation: Risk of torsades de pointes
  • Heart failure: Reduced cardiac contractility
  • Hypotension: Vascular smooth muscle effects
  • Bradycardia: Conduction system depression

Specialist Consultations

  • Endocrinology: For PTH evaluation and management
  • Nephrology: If renal involvement suspected
  • Cardiology: If significant arrhythmias present
  • Critical Care: If ICU monitoring required

Prevention Strategy

  • Weekly monitoring: Serum calcium levels during treatment
  • Adequate vitamin D: Target 25-OH vitamin D >30 ng/mL
  • Magnesium repletion: Maintain Mg >1.8 mg/dL
  • Patient education: Recognize early symptoms

When to Call 911

Immediate emergency care if: Seizures, difficulty breathing, severe muscle spasms, chest pain, loss of consciousness, or any signs of tetany. Do not attempt to drive - call emergency services immediately.

  • Gait disturbance: Unsteady walking, falls

  • Severe mood changes: Depression, psychosis
  • Autonomic symptoms: Bladder/bowel dysfunction
  • Visual disturbances: Blurred vision, diplopia

Potential Underlying Causes

  • Functional B12 deficiency: Despite normal serum levels
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Diabetic, autoimmune, toxic
  • Thyroid disorders: Hypo/hyperthyroidism
  • Autoimmune: Multiple sclerosis, lupus
  • Nutritional: Thiamine, folate, copper deficiency
  • Medication-induced: Metformin, PPIs, others

  • Clinical assessment: Detailed neurologic exam
  • Electrophysiology: EMG/NCS for neuropathy
  • Imaging: Brain/spine MRI if indicated
  • CSF analysis: If inflammatory process suspected

  • Functional B12: Methylmalonic acid, homocysteine
  • Thyroid function: TSH, Free T4, Free T3
  • Autoimmune panel: ANA, anti-dsDNA, RF
  • Trace elements: Copper, zinc levels
  • Diabetes screening: HbA1c, glucose tolerance
  • Vitamin levels: Thiamine, folate, vitamin E

  • Despite high B12: 852 pg/mL (elevated)

  • Duration: Weekly x4, then monthly

  • Endocrinology: If thyroid/diabetes suspected

  • Motor assessment: Strength, tone, reflexes
  • Coordination: Finger-to-nose, heel-to-shin

  • Cognitive screening: MMSE or MoCA

  • Sensory symptoms: First to improve (2-4 weeks)
  • Motor function: Gradual improvement (4-8 weeks)
  • Cognitive issues: May take 8-12 weeks
  • Mood symptoms: Often improve with energy levels

Neurology Consultation Goals

Comprehensive assessment for: Peripheral neuropathy, central nervous system involvement, functional vitamin deficiencies, and development of appropriate treatment strategies for neurologic recovery.
